Tier 1 Fleet pains — what the squadron feels visible, physical, day-to-day
Disparate point solutions, accumulated over years →A consolidated fleet, fielded and managed as one

What they told us is broken

Disparate point solutions, accumulated over years.Squadrons have a drawer of vendor-specific kit from a decade of one-off procurements. Nothing talks to anything else. Fifty squadrons, fifty microclimates.

Why our positioning is the right answer

We don't add another box. We consolidate the training posture onto a single device-agnostic platform already fielded at 80+ installations. One managed network, not one more vendor.

What they're buying

A consolidated fleet, fielded and managed as one.Not fifty procurements of fifty boxes. One action, one network, one vendor accountable for kit across every squadron. The hardware is ours — squadrons don't carry it, AFSFC doesn't refresh it. You're buying the consolidation, not the components.

Uneven baseline across the fleet →A single Day One baseline, held current for you

What they told us is broken

Uneven baseline across the fleet.Some sites have current kit, some are two refresh cycles behind. Whatever a squadron got in 2019 is still what they have in 2026. Procurement cycles can't level this.

Why our positioning is the right answer

Hardware-as-a-Service. We own it, we refresh it, we retire it. Squadrons don't carry it on their asset register, AFSFC doesn't run fifty hardware actions. Currency is our problem.

What they're buying

A single Day One baseline across every squadron, held current for you.You're not buying equipment — you're buying the guarantee that every squadron starts on the same footing and stays there. Refresh cycles stop being a procurement headache because they stop being yours.

Tier 2 Signal pains — what the enterprise can't see or act on systemic, hidden, cumulative
No visibility into what's actually happening in training →A continuous readiness signal from every squadron back to you

What they told us is broken

No visibility into what's actually happening in training.Events occur at the squadron and stay there. AFSFC can't answer "is the force ready against a Group 2 UAS at a given installation right now?" — not for C-UAS, not for most mission sets.

Why our positioning is the right answer

Every lane is a sensor. Scored reps produce structured evidence that flows back to HQ as it occurs, mapped to task, doctrine, standard, airman. Readiness becomes a signal you consume, not a report someone builds.

What they're buying

A continuous readiness signal from every squadron back to you.Not a quarterly report someone assembles — a live picture of who's qualified, on what task, to what standard, with the evidence behind every record. You stop asking squadrons to tell you about readiness. You see it.

Inconsistency between units in what's trained and how it's measured →Force-wide standardization without running a force-wide program

What they told us is broken

Inconsistency between units in what's trained and how it's measured.Two squadrons with "the same" capability run different scenarios and produce incomparable outputs. No one can say whether defenders at Lackland perform the same as defenders at Minot.

Why our positioning is the right answer

Common platform, common doctrine-aligned mission content, common scoring logic. Same task, same standard, same signal — everywhere. Standardization is a feature of the subscription, not a program to go run.

What they're buying

Force-wide standardization without running a force-wide program.Same task, same scoring, same signal at every squadron — guaranteed by construction, not enforced by policy. You're buying the comparability that makes cross-site readiness judgments defensible.

No mechanism to update the fleet universally when doctrine or threat changes →A standing update pipe from doctrine to every squadron

What they told us is broken

No mechanism to update the fleet universally when doctrine or threat changes.When AFJQS shifts, when a new UAS surfaces, when a TTP evolves — there's no pipe that gets the update into every squadron's training at the same time. Drift is the default.

Why our positioning is the right answer

Two-way connectivity. We push content and standards updates from our side, compliant, versioned, and consistent across all 50 squadrons in one motion. The recurring subscription is the update pipe.

What they're buying

A standing update pipe from doctrine to every squadron.When the threat changes or the standard moves, the force moves with it — in weeks, not years, without a new procurement action. You're buying the mechanism that keeps the force current, not a snapshot of today's content.

Tier 3 Institutional pains — what AFSFC can't do as the requirements owner authority-level, only Nelson sees these clearly
No bridge from training rep to live-fire qualification →Range time protected for airmen who've already proven they're ready

What they told us is broken

No bridge from training rep to live-fire qualification.Live range time is scarce, oversubscribed, and expensive. Today there's no quality gate — airmen go to the range whether they're prepared or not, and the range gets burned on reps that shouldn't have been taken.

Why our positioning is the right answer

Virtual readiness as the gate to live fire. Scored sim reps produce the evidence that an airman is ready for the range. Live fire is reserved for the final validation, not skill acquisition. Cable's "gate to live fire" made institutional.

What they're buying

Range time protected for airmen who've already proven they're ready.You're buying the gate that turns scarce live fire from a training resource into a qualification event. Every trip to the range is a validation, not a first attempt. The math on range utilization changes in your favor.

No credential at the end of the rep →Credentialed readiness, backed by evidence, at the airman level

What they told us is broken

No credential at the end of the rep.Training today produces "participation" — someone sat through it — not "readiness." AFJQS and CADMS exist to close this gap; nothing fielded today actually feeds them credentialed evidence.

Why our positioning is the right answer

Every scored rep produces an auditable, standards-mapped artifact that can feed a credential. AFSFC becomes the credentialing authority it's already supposed to be, with evidence backing every record.

What they're buying

Credentialed readiness, backed by evidence, at the airman level.Not participation records — qualifications. AFSFC becomes the institutional authority that actually credentials the force, because for the first time there's evidence behind every record. You're buying the authority structure you were always supposed to have.

No AFSFC input into the content and direction of training experiences →A seat at the design table for every mission pack the force receives

What they told us is broken

No AFSFC input into the content and direction of training experiences the force receives.AFSFC is the institutional requirements owner but has no seat at the table when training content gets built. Vendors build what they build; units take what they're given.

Why our positioning is the right answer

AFSFC as co-author, not recipient. The partnership model puts Nelson's team in the design loop for every mission pack on a defined cadence. Equities are in the product, not adjacent to it.

What they're buying

A seat at the design table for every mission pack the force receives.Your doctrine, your priorities, your sign-off — built into the governance cadence, not bolted on at the end. You're buying authorship of what the force trains on, not just the right to review it.

Every vendor needs a fresh ATO, so the enterprise data layer never materializes →The network authorization that unlocks everything else on this sheet

What they told us is broken

Every vendor needs a fresh ATO, so the enterprise data layer never materializes.Fielded systems don't touch the AF network. Without that, no evidence flows, no enterprise dashboard, no cross-site comparison. This is the hidden reason every other pain on this list persists.

Why our positioning is the right answer

Authority to Operate, achieved once, inherited by every task order. The network authorization is the unlock for every signal-layer benefit above it — without it, the rest is marketing.

What they're buying

The network authorization that unlocks everything else on this sheet.Done once, inherited by every task order after. Without it, none of the signal flows, none of the visibility materializes, none of the credentialing scales. You're buying the foundation that makes the rest real — and you're buying it once.

The reframe in one line

They walked in thinking about hardware and simulators. We want them walking out thinking about consolidation, signal, and institutional authority — so the CLIN structure and pricing that follow land as answers to problems they already named, not line items on a bill.

What Kelli holds back

Sustainment tail (who pays for content currency in Year 2+) isn't on this sheet. It's our pain, not theirs — surfacing unprompted invites scrutiny. Hybrid answer is ready if Nelson or Mostowski asks "what happens after Year 1."
